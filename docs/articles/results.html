Results

 

            So far, the output is reverberant, but no luck on getting the sound to appear where I want it.  Let’s look at what I was able to obtain.  First is the ‘tzzz’ sound by my roommate.  It is clear that it has echo (maybe a little too much).  But it sounds as if it’s coming right out of the headphones.  Unfortunately, the original was not anechoic.  So I generated a 250 Hz pure tone and ran it through the algorithm.  The results were interesting.  First, by looking at figure 1, you can see there is an initial start up time for the audio as it fills the room.  According to Dr. Moore, this is the way real rooms operate.  However, it seems the amount of time it takes to start up is exaggerated (nearly half a second for the left side).  Second, when you listen to the sound, there is a thud at the beginning.  I don’t think this thud would be in a real room.  I have a few possible explanations.  1) It may be a simple bug in the early echo response.  2) It may be the echo is over exaggerated, possibly due to the room size being too large or calculation errors.  3) A real room absorbs audio power when it bounces off the wall, unlike this room simulation.  But, to me, it sounds as if the thud is off in the distance somewhere; although, I can’t tell where.  Also, the right and left sides are considerably different.

 

            For the sample audio files, I used an outer room of 22 meters with the speakers four meters apart (2 meters to the left and right of the center).  The virtual source was 2 meters left of the left speaker and 7 meters behind the left speaker.  There are some other virtual source positions commented out in the source code.  An improvement would be to have the script find the distances from the room size, speaker positions, and virtual source location.
